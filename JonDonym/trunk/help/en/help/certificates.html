JonDonym certificates and their meaning

Certificates are used to identify operators of JonDonym services. Typically, a mix cascade consists of two or more consecutive mix servers operated by different organizations. Only if all of these operators cooperate could the anonymity of users be revoked. Users of JonDo should consider for themselves whether they trust the operators of a cascade and whether they connect to the service. It is therefore crucial that

  • the individual mixes are run by independent organizations and that
  • the information about these organizations is reliable.

Certificates are part of the so-called cryptographic public key method: the organization identified by a certificate owns a private key that it uses to create digital signatures. This key must always be kept secret since everyone could otherwise use it to create signatures. By comparision to the openly available public key others can check these signatures and verify that they really communicate with the requested organization. The correlation of identity and public key is proven by a digital certificate. This is an electronic document digitally signed by a certification authority. It is highly recommended to inspect the certificates of each individual server of a mix cascade (click on the different mix icons and then on the respective certificate).

Certification authorities

The JonDonym service may be compromised by the operator if a mix cascade is only nominally run by different operators, while in fact all mixes are under the control of a single organization. Trustworthy certification authorities (CAs) should ensure that operators with different names really are independent entities. Without an identity check by one of these authorities mixes are useless for JonDo. But in the end, you will also have to trust the certification authorities themselves, as they could in theory invent non-existent operators and thereby run mixes under false flag.

In JonDonym, there currently are only two root certification authorities (JonDos GmbH and TU Dresden JAP Team), and they have committed themselves to be very careful when certifying operators. Moreover, the certification of mix operators may also be done by so-called Sub-CAs, whose identity has been confirmed by the root CAs and who may perform their own certification processes. Sub-CAs are needed if the identity of operators known to the Sub-CA can not be confirmed by the root CA beyond doubt while such operators shall nevertheless not be excluded from the JonDonym system. In the end, the user himself will have to decide whether he wants to trust the Sub-CAs or not.

In the future, it is planned to perform multi-party-certification. By then at least two certification authorities will be needed in order to issue a trustworthy certificate. Thus, there will no longer be the need to trust any single certification authority. With three or more certification authorities, even if the secret certificate of a certification authority gets lost or is invalid in any way, all other certificates may remain valid.

Mix certificates and operator certificates

Both mixes and operators of mixes receive certificates. A CA issues an operator certificates that are attached to its identity. An operator may then generate as many mix certificates as he likes with his certificate. These mixes, or mix certificates respectively, can then be associated with their operators beyond a doubt. But it also means that information about the location of mixes is in the end certified by an operator himself and is therefore not as reliable as the information about the operator himself.

Statuses of certificates

A certificate is considered as verified only if you trust the certification authority who has issued this certificate. Non-verifiable certificates are generally not trusted as anyone who knows a little about computer technology can generate such certificates by himself. The validity of a certificate depends on the time period for which the certificate has been issued. Typically, you should not trust an expired certificate any longer, as it is not clear whether the owner still has the right to offer the service. Certificates may also be revoked, for example if their encryption was broken, the operator turned out to be dubious or if the private certificate got into the hands of unauthorized persons.

JonDo does not permit connections to mix cascades with at least one untrusted mix at the moment. Expired or revoked certificates are currently ignored, but are displayed as such. We are currently also implementing a cascade filter with a better scalability.
